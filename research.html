Gravitational waves

Gravitational waves are ripples in the fabric of space and time caused by accelerating masses, revealing new details about the most extreme cataclysms in the universe. Starting with the recent detection of binary black hole merger events GW150914 and GW151226 by LIGO, we have entered a brand new era of observational gravitational wave astronomy.

Data Analysis

LIGO data are remarkably similar to a typical audio stream: there is significant dynamic range in both frequency (20 Hz to 8 kHz) and amplitude (~10-24 to 10-21). Localization of a given signal on the sky is done by first triangulating between the Hanford, Livingston, and Virgo detectors, and then by comparing the coherent network sensitivity to signals of a given amplitude. Electromagnetic counterparts of merger events begin to flare up almost immediately, so calibration, detection, and localization must all be done in real-time.
